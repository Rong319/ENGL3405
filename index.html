02 RHETORICAL POSITION

THE SPORT ALREADY HAS A SCORING SYSTEM BUILT ON AUTHORSHIP.

ITS BROADCAST COVERAGE DOES NOT YET KNOW HOW TO SAY SO.

The China Mermaid Open scores every performance across four dimensions — movement completion, technical difficulty, performance expression, and creative choreography — with nearly fifty individually valued elements. Since 2025, a dedicated art panel evaluates choreography, artistic expression, and cultural integration separately.

The rules already treat athletes as authors.

Current broadcast coverage doesn't use that language.

It teaches audiences to feel before it gives them anything to read. Work titles become mood cues. Scores become verdicts. The athlete's compositional choices disappear into atmosphere.

Pressure Line broadcasts what the scoring system already describes.

Introduction
Introduction

Pressure Line covers sport the way it actually is — not the way it photographs.

We start with what the performance is trying to do — not what it looks like. Commentary names the work and explains the construction. Camera follows the athlete's structure. Editing serves the choreography. Everything else is secondary.

The China Mermaid Open is where we apply that standard. It runs more than ten legs a season, draws athletes from ten countries, and since 2025 has a dedicated art panel evaluating choreography, artistic expression, and cultural integration. The sport already knows how to read what athletes build. Current broadcast language often doesn't. Closing that gap is the reason this guide exists.

Movement Completion

Technical Difficulty

PL APPROACH

Performance Expression

Creative Choreography

PL APPROACH
We name the work's theme and cultural references before the first descent. The audience has a frame.

Section I
We Reject the Fantasy Frame

Before the competition begins, current coverage has already made its most consequential decision: it tells the audience how to feel, not how to judge. That instruction doesn't arrive in live commentary — it arrives in the language used to name and introduce the event. Once that frame is installed, the audience can no longer read a title as argument, a score as record, or choreography as structure. What the judging system treats as meaningful becomes unreadable.

FIG.2 · SHANGHAI MUNICIPAL INFORMATION OFFICE · 2024

The fantasy frame is institutional, not incidental.

For broadcasters: your opening line either repeats this instruction or interrupts it. There is no third option.
↗ FIG.2 · Shanghai Municipal Information Office · en.shio.gov.cn · 2024

The 2024 Shanghai Haichang leg was officially titled "Mermaid Fantasy Journey" in government-affiliated reporting. Athletes were described as gathering to "embark on a magical mermaid journey." This is not one correspondent's word choice. This is the event's official identity.

"Journey" isn't neutral. It swaps a competition for an experience — and tells the audience, before anything starts, that feeling is the right response here, not judging. By the time commentary begins, the fantasy frame is already the water everyone is swimming in.

FIG.3 · 2022 CHINA MERMAID OPEN · NANCHANG LEG · LIVE BROADCAST

The title is the athlete's first statement. Current coverage turns it into mood.

For broadcasters: the moment you receive a work title, you have one job — turn it into a reading frame before the athlete descends. Not a mood. A frame.
FIG.3 · 2022 China Mermaid Open · Nanchang Leg · Live Broadcast

At the 2022 Nanchang leg, the announcer names the work title — then immediately pivots to atmospheric language. No concept explained. No connection to the judging criteria. The title becomes a mood cue instead of an interpretive anchor.

Those two seconds are the most consequential decision in the broadcast. A work title names what the entire performance is organized around. When the announcer converts it into atmosphere instead, the audience gets a name for a work they'll never be given the tools to read.

Supporting evidence — same default in print

A Xinhua-linked report on Che Ranjun's 2022 Sanya Grand Final opens: "underwater ballet with the fish." The headline had promised to go "beyond beauty." The lead sentence reinstalled beauty as the primary frame before the first paragraph ended. Sentence order is a statement of priority — and the spectacle default wins every time there isn't a system to interrupt it.

↗ FIG.4 · Guangming Wang · Xinhua · gmw.cn · December 2022
FIG.5 · 163.COM · FIGURE SKATING COMMENTARY CRITICISM

The audience is already ready. The broadcast hasn't caught up.

For broadcasters: the audience doesn't need to be taught to care about this sport. They need to be given something to read.

Viewer criticism of CCTV5 figure skating commentary named the failure precisely: the commentator "just reads prepared lines," "doesn't recognize the athletes," and therefore "doesn't know how to evaluate today's and past performances." This example isn't here to compare mermaid competition to figure skating. It's here to establish one thing: the audience expectation already exists. Chinese viewers of athletic-artistic sport already know what a serious commentary standard looks like, and they'll name its absence when they see it.

↗ FIG.5 · 163.com · Figure Skating Commentary Criticism

Mermaid competition hasn't received that standard yet — not because the audience isn't ready, but because the broadcast language hasn't met them at the level the sport deserves. A competition with four scoring dimensions, nearly fifty evaluated elements, and a dedicated art panel is not an aquarium show. The audience knows it. The coverage doesn't act like it.

This is not a difference in eloquence. It is a difference in what the audience is being instructed to do — receive atmosphere, or read a work.

Section II
Our Position on the Sport

Section I showed what the fantasy frame costs: the audience loses the work. This section shows what's already available to replace it. The scoring system has a language for reading this sport — it's been there since the rulebook was written. The three examples below show what happens when you actually use it.

THE STANDARD ALREADY EXISTS

Before turning to mermaid competition, it matters to establish that this standard already exists in practice. Pressure Line isn't inventing a better standard. It's applying one that authored sport already uses — and that this sport's own community already applies in practice.

Theme named. Structure explained. Technical score (277.29) and artistic score (121.60) separated. This is not special treatment for an Olympic final. This is the minimum the scoring system deserves.

↗ olympics.com · Paris 2024 · China Artistic Swimming

Two freediving coaches analyzing Yang Lu's 2024 Asian Cup performance in real time — naming movements by technical term, citing scoring criteria, specifying difficulty tiers. This standard already exists within the sport community. Pressure Line brings it to broadcast.

↗ Douyin · v.douyin.com/h0BOWhjhr6Y/
Score records structure, not just excellence
WANG JIANPING 王建萍
2024 Shanghai Haichang Leg · 204.37 pts
WHAT CURRENT COVERAGE SAID
"She was originally a synchronized swimmer. Her choreography and artistic expression were outstanding, demonstrating a high level of professionalism."
WHAT IT FAILED TO EXPLAIN

That's a source, not an explanation. It tells the audience where capability came from. It doesn't tell them what the scoring system found, or what to look for in anyone else's performance.

WHAT THE SCORING SYSTEM IS ASKING

The creative choreography dimension scores internal logic — do movements set something up and resolve it? Does the performance have phrase structure, or just impressive elements? The 45-point gap is the panel's record of what they found when they applied that criterion. It's not a confirmation she was exceptional. It's a measurement of a specific structural quality almost no one else in the field was building at.

↗ FIG.9 · 163.com · Wang Jianping 2024 Shanghai Haichang score report
PRESSURE LINE
"Wang Jianping builds sequences that finish. Watch how each descent sets something up — and resolves it before she surfaces. That's phrase structure. That's what the 45-point gap is recording."
For broadcasters: When a score gap appears, find the dimension that produced it and explain the structural quality it measured. Background is a source. The scoring criterion is the explanation.
Choreography as translation problem the audience can watch for
GUO KE 郭克
2024 Asian Cup Grand Final · 《英歌舞》Yingge Dance
WHAT CURRENT COVERAGE SAID
"Distinctive." A placement recorded. The tradition named. The argument never explained.
WHAT IT FAILED TO EXPLAIN

"Distinctive" is an impression. It tells the audience something unusual happened without naming what problem the athlete set for himself — or giving them any way to evaluate whether he solved it.

WHAT THE SCORING SYSTEM IS ASKING

Guo Ke's 2024 Asian Cup Grand Final routine was built around the Yingge dance — a warrior percussion tradition from Guangdong, listed on China's national intangible cultural heritage register. The Yingge runs on three physical principles: weight (movements are grounded, gravity-driven, directed downward), percussion (rhythm comes from physical impact), and collective force (a warrior tradition choreographed for groups). All three are in direct structural tension with what underwater movement can do. A monofin generates horizontal propulsion. Buoyancy resists gravitational weight. Breath-hold eliminates percussive exertion. Solo competition removes the collective dimension. The creative choreography panel is evaluating one question: did the translation work?

↗ FIG.7 · Chinanews · Guo Ke 2024 Asian Cup Grand Final report
PRESSURE LINE
"Guo Ke has built this around the Yingge dance — weight, percussion, downward force. Watch whether the movement has intention going down, or just drift going up. Watch whether it feels struck, or merely swayed. That's the question."
For broadcasters: Name the tradition, define the translation tension it creates, and give the audience two or three physical signs to watch for. That's the difference between an impression and a reading frame.
Title is the athlete's argument — broadcast must hold it in place
HONG XUHUA 洪雪华
2024 Shanghai Haichang · 《青龙舞》Azure Dragon Dance
WHAT CURRENT COVERAGE SAID
"Wearing a long, flowing blue mermaid costume, Hong Seol-hwa, with her blonde hair, gracefully swayed and danced in the water, resembling a lithe mermaid."
WHAT IT FAILED TO EXPLAIN

The title is "Azure Dragon Dance." The coverage described her as a mermaid — the exact image she was working against.

WHAT THE SCORING SYSTEM IS ASKING

In Chinese cultural symbolism, the dragon is the opposite of the mermaid — power over grace, force over flow. An athlete who names her routine after a dragon inside a mermaid competition is making an argument with her title. She's saying this performance will push against the competition's default aesthetic, not inside it. The art panel evaluates whether the choreography and expression deliver on that argument. Current coverage received the title, noted her hair color, and described her as a lithe mermaid — the exact figure the title was refusing. The broadcast didn't miss the work. It rewrote it.

↗ FIG.8 · Sina Finance · Hong Xuhua Azure Dragon Dance report
PRESSURE LINE
"The title is 'Azure Dragon Dance.' The dragon is not the mermaid. Watch whether this performance carries force or grace. That's what the title is claiming. Hold that question."
For broadcasters: The title is not a label. It is the first claim the performance has to deliver on. Your job is to hold that claim in front of the audience long enough for them to evaluate whether the performance answers it.
"A score named without explanation is a fact.
A score explained is an argument."

Section III
How We Broadcast

These are not suggestions. They are Pressure Line's operating defaults — the direct consequences of what Sections I and II established. The commentary standard you saw in Section II is the baseline. Everything below is how you execute it.

Commentary
Name the work. Explain the construction. Make the score legible.
1
Before every descent: name the work, state the concept, identify what the panel is evaluating.

A title is not a mood cue — it's the frame. If you haven't named the concept before the athlete enters the water, the audience has already lost it.

2
Name every significant movement by its technical term, then explain what the athlete is managing at the same time.

"Tail-up rotation" names it. "Ankle above the head at mid-descent, breath reserve at its lowest" explains what the athlete is actually managing. The audience can't read difficulty as achievement unless they understand what the difficulty is.

3
When the score appears, name the dimension and explain the structural quality it measured.

Background tells where capability came from. The scoring criterion explains what the panel actually found.

4
Use silence when the performance should lead.

Continuous narration during a descent fills the space that belongs to the work. Watch, then interpret.

5
Before every surface return, tell the audience what comes next and how it connects to what has already been built.

Recovery is preparation. Every descent should begin with a reading frame, not just a breath.

Camera
Frame the performance condition. Not the environment.
1
One wide establishing shot at the start. After that, leave it unless the spatial condition becomes newly relevant.

The wide shot makes depth and scale legible. Once it's done that job, returning to it is a spectacle decision.

2
Medium frame — crown to monofin, water visible — is the primary frame during descent sequences. If you cannot justify leaving it in compositional terms, stay in it.

This is the only position where labor, line, and structure are simultaneously readable.

3
Marine life is environment, not content. Cut to it only if it directly clarifies something about the performance condition.

Cutting to fish during a descent tells the audience the interesting thing is the environment, not the athlete.

4
Close-ups show what the athlete is choosing to make visible — not what catches the light.

A close-up is justified when expression is doing compositional work — when the art panel would score it.

Editing
Every cut is an editorial interpretation. Make sure it's yours — not the default.
1
Cut at structural breaks — not visual opportunities. If a cut is motivated by visual excitement rather than structural transition, it is almost certainly the wrong cut.

A mid-sequence cut destroys the compositional argument the athlete has been building.

2
In team events, hold the full-width frame through synchronized sequences.

Synchronization is a scored criterion. A cut to one face during a synchronized sequence is editorially irresponsible.

3
No atmospheric inserts during active performance.

Tank wide-shots, fish cutaways, crowd reactions during descents — these all tell the audience the interesting thing is the atmosphere, not the work.

Guo Ke asked whether a warrior tradition built on ground percussion and downward force could survive translation into breath-hold propulsion through water. Che Ranjun asked whether a piece built around the cost of perfection could hold that tension across six descents without hearing the music. Hong Xuhua named her routine after a dragon. Power over grace, force over flow. She asked whether the performance could make that argument visible. Wang Jianping brought the phrase structure of synchronized swimming into a scoring context that had never seen it, and the judges put 45 points between her and the field to say so.

What links these performances is not beauty, but authorship: each athlete set a problem, built a structure, and submitted that structure to judgment. Current coverage called the results beautiful. That's not wrong — but it's a description of a different thing. Beauty is what the performance looks like from the outside. Pressure Line broadcasts what it is doing on the inside.

The name carries three meanings that are present in every performance. There is the physical pressure of depth — the water the athletes are working through. There is the physiological pressure of breath-hold — the state in which every scored element is executed. And there is the interpretive pressure that every broadcast decision applies: whether the language you use pushes the audience toward reading the work, or toward consuming the image. Pressure Line means making every broadcast decision push in one direction — toward reading.

The rulebook already knows how to read this sport. The broadcast has been lagging behind it. Pressure Line closes that gap.

Name the work.
Explain the construction.
Make the score legible.
That is the standard.
