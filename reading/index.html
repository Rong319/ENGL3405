CRITICAL READING · 01

After Gold, Still Asked to Be a Woman

On August 1, 2021, Gong Lijiao won China's first Olympic gold in a field event. In the interview after the victory, CCTV wanted to know when she would lose weight.

PRESSURE LINE  ·  MAY 2026  ·  8 MIN READ

20.58 meters: a personal best, a national first, and the moment before the broadcast changed the subject.

SECTION 00 00

The Throw

Gong Lijiao threw 20.58 meters. That should have been enough to hold the room for a while. It was China's first Olympic gold in any field event, and it came after a long wait: not just for the country, but for Gong herself. Tokyo was her fourth Olympic Games. She had taken bronze in Beijing, silver in London, and fourth in Rio before finally reaching the top of the podium. By then, she was already a two-time world champion. The Tokyo throw was not the beginning of her story. It was the moment a long athletic argument finally landed.

Shot put is easy to misunderstand because it looks like one explosion of strength. A great throw is not strength alone. It is timing, balance, footwork, release angle, body memory, and a calm kind of violence. The ball leaves the hand in one second, but that second is full of work.

World Athletics understood the moment that way. Its report led with the distance, the personal best, the dominance of the performance, and the history of the win. Gong's own words after the event stayed inside the sport too. She said she had "given everything" and that the gold medal had been something she had wanted for years. The athletic meaning was already there.

Then came the post-victory interview.

This is where the profile begins. Not with Gong's whole life story, and not even with the full history of Chinese shot put. The stronger question is smaller and sharper: what did the interview do to the win?

What CCTV did after the throw was not just sexism. It was substitution: the interview replaced one story with another. It moved the victory away from force, technique, and history, and toward weight, marriage, and womanhood — exposing a habit in which women in masculine-coded sports are celebrated only after their femininity has been put back on trial.

This is the question Pressure Line was built to ask. In our Guidebook, we named three ways broadcast language fails athletes: by omission, by reduction, and by substitution. The Gong interview is substitution at its most exposed.

SECTION 01 01

The Substitution

I have watched that interview many times now, and the part that does not get easier is how friendly it sounds. The reporter is not yelling. She is not trying to sound cruel. The reporter is being chatty — asking the kinds of questions a relative asks at dinner: when are you going to lose some weight, do you have a boyfriend yet, when will you finally settle down.

The questions belong to a script that runs at every Chinese family gathering I can remember: the aunt who comments on your weight before saying hello, the uncle who asks if you are seeing anyone, everyone leaning in, everyone thinking they are just showing concern. CCTV did not write that script. It just turned the camera on while it ran.

That is why the interview matters rhetorically. It did not attack Gong directly. It changed the subject so smoothly that the change almost sounded normal. The victory was still there, but the interview made another performance more urgent. Gong had already performed as an athlete. Now she was being asked to perform legible femininity.

The reporter called her a "manly woman" because of shot put. She asked if Gong would lose weight. She asked about marriage. She asked whether Gong would "return to being herself." In English coverage, that phrase has often been translated as "return to being a woman," and that translation is useful. But the Chinese phrase is even sharper: 做回自己.

做回自己 literally means "return to being yourself." The important word is 回 — return. To return means you have left. The question assumes that the athletic Gong is a kind of departure, a temporary version produced by shot put, training, muscle, and competition. The feminine Gong is treated as the real home she should go back to.

So the reporter was not only asking what Gong would do next. She was asking when this Olympic champion would come back from the body that had just won.

CCTV's post-gold interview moved from the throw to Gong's body, weight, marriage, and womanhood.

SECTION 02 02

The Reading

A post-victory interview is not a neutral space. It is one of those moments when an athlete is still warm from the event, still breathing inside the result, and suddenly has to turn the performance into language. The athlete does not fully control the frame. The reporter arrives with the microphone, and the first question tells the audience what kind of moment this is going to be.

The CCTV interview made this a gender moment.

A better interview could have stayed with the sport. It could have asked about the 20.58 meters, the years between Rio and Tokyo, the technical changes that made the throw possible, or the feeling of finally winning after so many near misses. It could have kept the audience inside the athletic work. It can also move the audience somewhere else entirely.

CCTV moved somewhere else.

The arm-wrestling question shows how that movement worked. The reporter asked whether Gong arm-wrestled with men. On the surface, it sounded playful, like a joke about strength. But the joke placed Gong's body in a test. Was she too strong? Was she outside the ordinary category of woman? Could her strength be laughed at, domesticated, made less threatening?

Gong answered, "I am very gentle. I don't arm-wrestle."

Read it that way and the moment changes. That answer is not only politeness. It is a small refusal. She accepts the word "gentle," but she does not accept the performance the question is trying to produce. She does not give the reporter the strong-woman spectacle. She does not play along with the idea that her strength needs to be explained as unfeminine. She closes the question by using its own logic against it.

Watching this as a Chinese woman, the part that gets me is how recognizable it is. Women are often expected to keep the room comfortable while being made uncomfortable. They have to answer without sounding angry, refuse without looking rude, and somehow protect themselves gently. Gong did that in front of a national audience, right after winning an Olympic gold medal.

That does not make the interview harmless. It makes the pressure easier to see.

SECTION 03 03

The Pattern

The cultural context matters because Gong was not being interviewed by a random entertainment channel. She was a Chinese woman winning a historic Olympic gold on state television. National celebration did not protect her from gender policing. The greater the achievement, the more the interview seemed to need to pull her back into a familiar feminine script.

Xu, Fan, and Brown's 2021 study of sports gender typing in China helps explain why shot put matters here. Sports are not only played by men or women; they are also imagined as masculine or feminine before any athlete enters the field. Shot put sits on the masculine side of that cultural map. When a woman wins in a sport already coded as male, her body becomes something the broadcast seems to manage. The interview was that management.

Xu, Billings, and Fan's 2018 study of CCTV Olympic coverage also matters here. They found that female athletes were often framed through personality, appearance, and physical traits instead of technical labor. Gong's interview is not a separate problem. It is the same descriptor problem at maximum volume: a champion compressed into questions about her body, temperament, and marital future while the actual sport disappeared from the segment.

Sixth Tone reported that another Tokyo gold medalist, shooter Yang Qian, was asked by a CCTV reporter about her "ideal man." That detail matters because it shows the Gong interview was not a one-off mistake by one awkward reporter. It belonged to a broader broadcast habit. Even when women win, the interview can still pull them toward romance, appearance, and acceptability.

Two outlets covered the same six minutes after the throw. World Athletics led with the distance, the personal best, the historical first. CCTV led with "manly woman." Same medal, same body, same six minutes — and two completely different reports about what the moment meant.

The interesting question is not which one is correct. World Athletics is the federation; of course it stays with the sport. The harder question is why the home country's national broadcaster could not.

SECTION 04 04

The Refusal

That is the contested part of Gong's public image. CCTV constructed one version of her: the champion whose body still had to be made feminine again. But the interview did not stay inside CCTV's broadcast. It moved — first through Weibo screenshots and clips, then through Chinese news sites, and within days into English-language coverage by The China Project, South China Morning Post, and other outlets. By the time the backlash crested, the interview had been re-watched, re-quoted, and re-framed across two media systems and three languages.

That circulation matters because each retelling kept the questions visible. It also changed what the audience was looking at. At first, the interview framed Gong as the problem: too strong, too masculine, not yet returned to the correct version of womanhood. But once the clip circulated, the frame began to turn around. The questions themselves became the problem.

According to The China Project, the Weibo hashtag #女性能被谈论的只有婚姻吗# — "Is marriage the only thing we can discuss about women?" — was viewed more than 330 million times. The power of that hashtag is not only the number. It is the grammar. It is a question thrown back at the interview.

The public did not simply say, "This was sexist." It asked why the conversation around a woman had to end up at marriage at all. That question named the mechanism more clearly than a slogan could. It showed that viewers were not only offended by the tone of the interview. They recognized the substitution.

Peng, Wu, and Chen's 2024 analysis of Chinese online discussions about sportswomen helps explain why the backlash spread so quickly. They document a recurring pattern: even when female athletes perform exceptionally, public conversation often moves toward their bodies, attractiveness, dating lives, and social acceptability. The Gong backlash was powerful because people were not discovering that pattern for the first time. They were refusing it in public.

Gong's own answers matter for the same reason. She did not give a long feminist speech — her refusal was quieter and more uncomfortable than that. She kept answering, but her answers exposed the poverty of the questions. The interview asked for a woman who could be made smaller than her medal. Gong did not become that woman.

SECTION 05 05

What This Reveals

Pressure Line's reading begins with the substitution. In the China Mermaid Open guidebook, our main concern was that broadcast language often covers athletic labor with beauty. In mermaid competition, the athlete's authorship — the choice, revision, timing, and risk underneath the performance — can disappear into atmosphere. The work becomes grace. The construction becomes fantasy. The sport becomes something pretty to look at.

Gong Lijiao's case is not about aquatic sport, but it follows the same structure. What disappears is force, technique, training history, and national athletic meaning. What replaces it is weight, marriage, temperament, and womanhood.

Same structure, different mask.

This is why the profile belongs on Pressure Line. Our company is not only interested in what cameras show. We are interested in what coverage makes readable. In one sport, the replacement may look like beauty. In another, it may look like friendliness. In Gong's case, it sounded like concern. That is what made it hard to reject in the moment.

The South China Morning Post framed the story around CCTV being "under fire" after calling Gong a "manly girl" and asking when she would "return to being a woman." That framing was accurate, but it still stayed close to the offense. Pressure Line is interested in the structure behind the offense: how a gold-medal moment was taken away from the athlete and replaced with a test she should never have had to pass.

Gong Lijiao did not need that interview to make her victory meaningful. The meaning was already in the throw. The camera could see her — it was pointed straight at her. The problem was the script it had been handed: when a woman wins, ask when she will return to being one.

The throw was 20.58 meters.

The script was older than that.
